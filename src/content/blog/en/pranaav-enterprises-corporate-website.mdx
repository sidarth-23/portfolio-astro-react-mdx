Most side projects live on GitHub and never get used. This one is different.

**Pranaav Enterprises** is a civil construction services company in Bangalore, operating since 2000 — 25 years in the industry, with clients like Larsen & Toubro, Prestige Group, and Shapoorji Pallonji on their roster. They had the work history, but no web presence to match it. The brief was simple: give the business a credible face online, something a project manager could open on a phone and forward over WhatsApp.

This post is about how that brief turned into a production site — and what the client workflow taught me about shipping software for people who do not care about your stack.

---

## The First Conversation

The owner did not ask for a “website.” He asked for something he could send to prospects without embarrassment. That is a very different requirement.

In the Indian civil contracting market, trust moves fast and informally. A procurement officer gets a reference, Googles the name, and makes a first impression in seconds. If the search result is a blank LinkedIn page or a half-finished Justdial listing, the conversation stalls. If it is a clean, mobile-friendly site that explains services and shows serious clients, the conversation continues.

That became the real goal: **reduce friction in a referral conversation.** Not a brand statement. Not a CMS-powered platform. Just a link that closes the credibility gap before the first phone call.

---

## Choosing the Stack for Handoff

I went with **Next.js App Router**, **Tailwind CSS**, **TypeScript**, and **Vercel**. Here is why each mattered for this specific project:

- **Next.js** is common enough that any future developer can pick it up without learning a bespoke setup.
- **App Router** keeps pages as files with clear routes — `/`, `/customers`, `/contact-us` — which makes handoff and maintenance obvious.
- **Tailwind** let me move fast without fighting component styles, which matters when the client is iterating on copy and layout over WhatsApp voice notes.
- **`next/image` + Vercel’s CDN** handled the heavy lifting for service photos and client logos, so I did not have to build a separate image pipeline.
- **Vercel** meant deploys were git-push simple. When the client said “the phone number is wrong,” the fix was live in under a minute.

Could this have been built in Astro, SvelteKit, or plain HTML? Yes. The stack choice here was about maintainability and speed of iteration, not novelty.

---

## The Site Structure

I kept it to three pages on purpose. Fewer pages means fewer decisions for the visitor and fewer things to maintain for the client.

**Home (`/`)** — The full story in one scroll: the 25-year milestone, a tabbed services section, the client logo wall, and a WhatsApp CTA. The homepage answers “what do you do?” and “who trusts you?” before the user has to think about clicking anywhere.

**Our Clients (`/customers`)** — A dedicated page for the client roster. In enterprise B2B, logos carry weight. Having L&T, Shapoorji Pallonji, and Embassy on their own page, not buried mid-scroll, makes a difference when the site is being shown to a procurement team.

**Contact Us (`/contact-us`)** — Every contact path in one place: phone numbers, Gmail, Bangalore office address with Google Maps, and the WhatsApp button again. No forms. No ticket systems. Just the channels the business actually uses.

---

## The Services Section

Pranaav Enterprises does more than most people expect from a single contractor: water supply, excavation, demolition, debris cleaning, JCB and machine hire, ambulance service, asphalting, and layout development.

The challenge was presenting all of that without turning the homepage into a wall of text. I used a tabbed interface: each service gets its own tab with an image and a short description. Users can explore what is relevant to them without being overwhelmed by the full list.

## Design Decisions for a B2B Audience

Building for a civil contractor’s audience is different from building a consumer product. The visitors are project managers, procurement officers, and site engineers — not general consumers.

**Information density over aesthetics.** The site needs to answer the important questions in the first scroll. Whitespace is nice, but not when it buries the answer to “what do you do?”

**WhatsApp as the primary CTA.** In Indian B2B construction, WhatsApp is how business actually gets done. Every page has a floating WhatsApp button with a pre-filled message. That is not a workaround — it is the right UX for this audience.

**Mobile-first.** A contractor forwarding the link is almost certainly sending it to a mobile user. The layout is fully responsive with a hamburger nav on smaller screens.

**Real photography.** The service images are actual photos of the work. Stock construction photos would have undermined the exact credibility the site is trying to build.

## What Made This Different from a Side Project

A few things shift when you are shipping for a real business:

**Content is your responsibility too.** You do not get placeholder text. You have to extract service descriptions, gather client logos, confirm contact details, and make sure everything is accurate before it goes live. That is a different kind of work from writing components.

**The client does not care about your stack.** Whether it is Next.js, Astro, or anything else is invisible to Pranaav Enterprises. What they care about is: does it look professional, does it load fast, and can they send the link to L&T’s procurement team without hesitation. Every technical decision has to serve that outcome.

**Iteration happens fast.** Once the site is live and the client starts showing it around, feedback comes back quickly. “Can we add this service?” “Can we make the phone number bigger?” Being on Next.js + Vercel meant changes were a git push away from being live.

**The WhatsApp button has already driven real leads.** That is the metric that matters — not Lighthouse scores.

---

## Final Thoughts

There is a version of this project that could have been more technically interesting: a headless CMS, an admin dashboard, a booking system. But the right call was to keep it simple, ship it fast, and make sure the 25 years of work Pranaav Enterprises has done is represented online with the credibility it deserves.

Sometimes the best engineering decision is knowing what not to build.
